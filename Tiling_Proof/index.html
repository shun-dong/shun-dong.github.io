Project Overview

Fomin and Pylyavskyy [^FP23] developed a framework that unifies many incidence theorems in projective geometry. Instead of proving Pappus, Desargues, and related theorems separately, incidence conditions are encoded into tiled surfaces, and a global cancellation argument forces one incidence relation from the others.

The proposal states that their results can be generalized to incidence theorems in elliptic geometry and hyperbolic geometry. Elliptic geometry appears to give no new information, while in hyperbolic geometry two hyperbolic geodesics can meet in three different ways in the projective completion of the hyperbolic plane:

  • P: the geodesics meet inside the hyperbolic plane.
  • I: the geodesics meet at an ideal boundary point.
  • E: the geodesics meet outside the closed hyperbolic disk.

[!exercise] Main research question
Given a tiled surface, and given a label $P,I,$ or $E$ on each tile, when does this type assignment come from a genuine hyperbolic incidence configuration?

Furthermore, when do partial type assumptions force the type of a remaining tile, thereby producing a genuinely typed hyperbolic incidence theorem?

^exercise-main-research-question

The expected background is linear algebra, graph theory, and elementary projective geometry. No prior knowledge of hyperbolic geometry or the Fomin-Pylyavskyy theory is required.

Classical Motivation: Linear Incidence Theorems

Linear incidence theorems concern configurations of points, lines, and subspaces in projective spaces, viewed through their relative positions. A typical result asserts that a finite collection of incidence relations, together with suitable nondegeneracy assumptions, forces an additional incidence relation. Classical examples include Desargues’ theorem and Pappus’ theorem.

In Hilbert’s foundations of projective geometry [^Hil02], Desargues’ theorem is closely tied to associativity in projective coordinates, while Pappus’ theorem is similarly tied to commutativity. Modern incidence geometry also has effective verification procedures: Grassmann-Cayley algebras, elimination, and Gröbner-basis methods can turn many incidence proofs into mechanical calculations [^Stu91]; [^RG95]; [^LW01].

The central problem is therefore not only to list incidence theorems or prove them one by one, but to understand the structures organizing them. A topological answer was initiated by Richter-Gebert [^RG06], pursued by Glynn [^Gly13], Richter-Gebert-Li [^RGL19], Baralić-Curien-Milićević-Obradović-Petrić-Zekić-Živaljević [^BCM+20], and developed systematically by Fomin-Pylyavskyy [^FP23].

The master theorem of Fomin-Pylyavskyy associates linear incidence implications to quadrilateral tilings of closed connected oriented surfaces. This also suggests that an incidence theorem may carry a topological complexity: one can ask on which oriented surfaces the statement admits such a proof, and how the answer depends on the formulation. Since closed connected oriented surfaces are classified by genus, a natural invariant is the minimal genus of such a surface.

A Quick Start on Fomin-Pylyavskyy Theory

Let us consider a linear incidence theorem valid in $\mathbb{P}^2_k$ for some field $k$. Let $T$ be a bicolored quadrilateral tiling of a closed connected oriented surface. Black vertices are labeled by points of a projective plane, and white vertices are labeled by lines.

Each tile consists of two black vertices and two white vertices. If they are labeled by $A,B$ and $\ell,m$, respectively, the boundary of the tile is required to be
$$

A-\ell-B-m-A.

$$
Such a tile is denoted $S(A,B;\ell,m)$.

[!definition] Coherent tile
A tile $S(A,B;\ell,m)$ is called coherent if:

  1. neither $A$ nor $B$ lies on $\ell$ or $m$;
  2. either $A=B$ or $\ell=m$, or else the three points $A,B,\ell\cap m$ are collinear.

^definition-coherent-tile

[!theorem] Fomin-Pylyavskyy master theorem
Let $T$ be a bicolored quadrilateral tiling of a closed connected oriented surface, with black vertices labeled by points and white vertices labeled by lines in a projective plane. If all but one tiles are coherent, then the remaining tile is coherent as well.

^theorem-fomin-pylyavskyy-master

$Proof.$ The algebraic reason is cancellation of mixed cross-ratios. Choose vector representatives of the point labels and covector representatives of the line labels. For an edge $A-\ell$ on a tile, label it with the pairing $\langle A,\ell\rangle$. This pairing is defined in projective space up to nonzero scaling, and the nonincidence assumption makes it nonzero.

For each tile $S(A,B;\ell,m)$, set
$$

\chi(A,B;\ell,m):=
\frac{\langle A,\ell\rangle\langle B,m\rangle}
{\langle A,m\rangle\langle B,\ell\rangle}.

$$
This is a well-defined projective invariant. Moreover, the tile is coherent if and only if
$$

\chi(A,B;\ell,m)=1.

$$
Indeed, if $A\neq B$ and $\ell\neq m$, then the line $(AB)$ meets $\ell\cap m$ if and only if there is a nonzero vector of the form $\lambda A+\mu B$ annihilated by both $\ell$ and $m$, which is equivalent to
$$

\langle A,\ell\rangle\langle B,m\rangle-\langle A,m\rangle\langle B,\ell\rangle=0.

$$

Multiplying $\chi(A,B;\ell,m)$ over all tiles of $T$, each edge pairing appears once in the numerator and once in the denominator because the surface is oriented and adjacent tiles traverse an edge in opposite directions. Hence
$$

\prod_{\tau\in T}\chi(\tau)=1.

$$
If all but one tiles are coherent, all but one cross-ratios are $1$, so the remaining one is also $1$.

$\blacksquare$

The source notes that this master theorem plays the same role as Menelaus’ multiplicative cancellation in triangular-tiling proofs. The general version in dimension $n$ replaces “line” by “hyperplane” and has the same proof. The source contains an unresolved reference “Theorem??” at this point.

[!example] Desargues’ theorem
Let $a,b,c$ be distinct concurrent lines in the projective plane. Pick distinct points
$$

A_1,A_2\in a\setminus(b\cup c),\quad
B_1,B_2\in b\setminus(a\cup c),\quad
C_1,C_2\in c\setminus(a\cup b).

$$

Set
$$

A=(B_1C_1)\cap(B_2C_2),\quad
B=(A_1C_1)\cap(A_2C_2),\quad
C=(A_1B_1)\cap(A_2B_2).

$$

Then $A,B,C$ are collinear.

^example-desargues-theorem

For the tiling proof, let
$$

a=(A_1A_2),\quad b=(B_1B_2),\quad c=(C_1C_2),

$$
and
$$

a_1=(B_1C_1),\quad b_1=(A_1C_1),\quad c_1=(A_1B_1),

$$
$$

a_2=(B_2C_2),\quad b_2=(A_2C_2),\quad c_2=(A_2B_2).

$$
The six tiles in the sphere tiling encode six coherence conditions. The first five are immediate from the construction. For example, $a_1\cap c=C_1$, and $A_1,B,C_1$ are collinear because $B\in b_1=(A_1C_1)$. Since the product of the six mixed cross-ratios is $1$, the remaining cross-ratio is also $1$. Thus the last tile is coherent, so $B,C,a_1\cap a_2$ are collinear. Since $a_1\cap a_2=A$, this says exactly that $A,B,C$ are collinear.

[!example] Generic Pappus’ theorem
Let $P_1,\dots,P_6$ be six generic points in the projective plane. Then any two of the following three conditions imply the third:

  • A: $(P_1P_2),(P_3P_4),(P_5P_6)$ are concurrent.
  • B: $(P_2P_3),(P_4P_5),(P_6P_1)$ are concurrent.
  • C: $(P_1P_4),(P_2P_5),(P_3P_6)$ are concurrent.

^example-generic-pappus

For the tiling proof, define
$$

A=(P_3P_4)\cap(P_5P_6),\quad
B=(P_2P_3)\cap(P_1P_6),\quad
C=(P_1P_4)\cap(P_2P_5),

$$
and let
$$

a=(BC),\quad b=(AC),\quad c=(AB).

$$
Thus
$$

A=b\cap c,\quad B=a\cap c,\quad C=a\cap b.

$$
The nine tiles of the torus tiling encode the following nine coherence conditions:

  • $P_3,P_4,b\cap c$ are collinear.
  • $P_5,P_6,b\cap c$ are collinear.
  • $P_2,P_3,a\cap c$ are collinear.
  • $P_6,P_1,a\cap c$ are collinear.
  • $P_1,P_4,a\cap b$ are collinear.
  • $P_2,P_5,a\cap b$ are collinear.
  • $P_1,P_2,b\cap c$ are collinear.
  • $P_4,P_5,a\cap c$ are collinear.
  • $P_3,P_6,a\cap b$ are collinear.

The first six conditions hold by construction. The last three are exactly the three concurrency conditions in Pappus’ theorem. If any two of them hold, then eight of the nine tiles are coherent. Since the product of all nine mixed cross-ratios is $1$, the ninth is also $1$, giving the remaining concurrency condition.

The source notes that this generic version is not Pappus’ theorem in full generality, because the statement and proof require general-position assumptions. A tiling-based proof of the full theorem is given in [^PS26], Section 2.4.

From Projective Geometry to Hyperbolic Geometry

The main goal is to extend the tiling-based technique from projective geometry to other settings, including elliptic geometry, hyperbolic geometry, Möbius geometry, and perhaps other settings. This proposal discusses elliptic and hyperbolic geometry.

[!definition] Polarity
In both elliptic and hyperbolic cases, write $\beta$ for the relevant bilinear form. For a linear subspace $W\subset V$, define its $\beta$-polar subspace by
$$

W^\perp:={v\in V\mid\beta(v,w)=0\text{ for all }w\in W}.

$$

^definition-polarity

If $a=\mathbb{P}(E)$ and $b=\mathbb{P}(F)$ are complementary projective flats, with
$$

\dim E+\dim F=\dim V,

$$
then the basic incidence bracket may be written as
$$

[a,b]=\Omega(e_0\wedge\cdots\wedge e_r\wedge f_0\wedge\cdots\wedge f_s),

$$
where
$$

E=\operatorname{span}(e_0,\dots,e_r),\quad F=\operatorname{span}(f_0,\dots,f_s),

$$
and $\Omega\in\bigwedge^{n+1}V^*$ is a fixed volume form.

$$

[a,b]_\beta:=\det(\beta(e_i,n_j))_{0\leq i,j\leq r}.

$$

[!definition] Mixed cross-ratio for complementary flats
For four alternating complementary flats $a_1,a_2,a_3,a_4$, where $a_1,a_3$ have the same dimension and $a_2,a_4$ have the complementary dimension, define
$$

\operatorname{MCR}_\beta(a_1,a_3;a_2,a_4)
:=
\frac{[a_1,a_2]_\beta[a_3,a_4]_\beta}
{[a_3,a_2]_\beta[a_1,a_4]_\beta}.

$$

A quadrilateral tile is called coherent if
$$

\operatorname{MCR}_\beta(a_1,a_3;a_2,a_4)=1.

$$

Incidence Types in Hyperbolic Geometry

Compared to projective geometry, there is no new information in incidence theorems in elliptic geometry. Thus the proposal focuses on hyperbolic geometry, beginning with dimension $2$.

In the Klein disk model of the hyperbolic plane, $\mathbb{H}^2$ is the interior of a disk, and hyperbolic geodesics are chords of the disk. Two hyperbolic geodesics do not always meet inside $\mathbb{H}^2$, but if the disk is viewed inside the projective plane, the projective lines containing the two chords always meet. Their intersection occurs in one of three regions.
取 $[x,y,z]\in\hat{\mathbb{H}}^2$, 在 Klein 圆盘上的投影是 $\kappa[x,y,z]=\left( \frac{x}{z}, \frac{y}{z} \right)$, projection to $\Pi:z=1$, 由于 $z^2=1+x^2+y^2$, $\left( \frac{x}{z} \right)^2+\left( \frac{y}{z} \right)^2=\frac{x^2+y^2}{z^2}<1$.
For geodesic $\gamma(t)$, $\kappa~\gamma(t)\in span\left{ \gamma(0),\dot\gamma(0) \right}\cap\Pi$ which is a line

[!definition] Hyperbolic geodesic intersection types
The three types are:

  • $P$: proper interior intersection.
  • $I$: ideal shared endpoint.
  • $E$: exterior outside intersection.

We use the notation
$$

P=\text{proper},\quad I=\text{ideal},\quad E=\text{exterior}.

$$

^definition-hyperbolic-incidence-types

In a hyperbolic version of a Fomin-Pylyavskyy tile
$$

f=S(b_1,b_2;w_1,w_2),

$$
the interpretation is:

  • the black vertices $b_1,b_2$ represent points in $H^2$;

  • the white vertices $w_1,w_2$ represent hyperbolic geodesics;

  • the two white geodesics meet at a projective point $C_f$;

  • the coherence condition says that the geodesic through $b_1$ and $b_2$ also passes through $C_f$ in the projective completion.

  • 对高维情况不再是测地线

Thus each coherent tile has a type
$$

\tau_f\in{P,I,E}.

$$
This extra type information is invisible in ordinary projective incidence geometry and elliptic geometry, but it is natural in hyperbolic geometry.

[!exercise] Problem 2: Typed Hyperbolic Incidence Theorem
When is $\operatorname{Comp}_{f^*}(\alpha)$ a singleton?

^exercise-typed-hyperbolic-incidence-theorem

If
$$

\operatorname{Comp}_{f^*}(\alpha)={\sigma},

$$
then the partial type assumptions force the conclusion tile to have type $\sigma$. This gives a typed hyperbolic incidence theorem. Ordinary Fomin-Pylyavskyy theory says that if all assumption tiles are coherent, then the conclusion tile is coherent. The new hyperbolic question asks whether the conclusion tile’s type is also forced.

[!remark] Non-uniqueness of conclusion type
It can be easily verified that the type of the conclusion tile is not always uniquely determined.

Pesudoline

[!definition] pseudoline
A line arrangement is the partition of the plane induced by a set of lines in the plane.
A pseudoline is a simple curve in the plane that goes to infinity in two directions. (In other words, a pseudoline is the image of a line under a homeomorphism of the plane.)
A collection of pseudolines is a set of pseudolines such that any two members of the set intersect at most once, and cross if they intersect.
A pseudoline arrangement is the partition of the plane induced by a collection of pseudolines.
A pseudoline arrangement is stretchable (or realizable) if there is an arrangement of lines with the same combinatorial structure.
A line or pseudoline arrangement is uniform if no three lines intersect in a point and no two lines are parallel.
[^sho91]

[!remark] The stretchability of a pseudoline arrangement can be formalized by a provability of a incidence theorem.
Suppose $L$ is a collection pseudoline, $P$ is the intersections of them, and $M$ be the relations of $L,P$ (which can be written as a matrix [^ps26]). Let $L^*=L\cup\set{l^*}$, $P^*=P\cup\set{p^*}$, $M^*=M\cup\set{p^\notin l^}$. Consider the incidence theorem $\forall L^*~P^*, M^\implies p^\in l^*$, which is true iff the pseudoline arrangement is not stretchable.

Reference

  • The original Fomin-Pylyavskyy paper[^FP23] should be read carefully; it systematically develops tiling-based proofs and the combinatorial interpretations behind them.
  • Pylyavskyy and Skopenkov[^PS26] formalize tiling proofs, study field-dependent incidence theorems, and show that a sphere-tiling proof implies division-ring universality.
  • Kühne and Larson[^KL25] show that a field-universal incidence theorem with a one-step surface-tiling proof has a commutative-ring-universal version, and give a field-universal theorem without such a one-step proof.
  • Izosimov[^Izo26] generalizes the tiling-based techniques to division rings and arbitrary rings.
  • Katzenberger and Richter-Gebert[^KRG26] relate provability of the master theorem to Menelaus’ theorem.
  • Zhang[^Zha] studies in what sense, and for which field-universal incidence statements, a genus-$g$ surface tiling gives a proof.
  • Izosimov and Pylyavskyy[^IP25] study the dimer model and dynamical incidence geometry; this is adjacent background rather than a core project reference.
